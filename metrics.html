Topline

These are your size & momentum numbers — how many people, how much they do, and how much they spread the app. They tell you the app is alive, but not whether it's sticky — that's the retention section below, which matters far more this early.

Engagement quality — outlier check

What it is: how the total events spread across people. Mean vs median is the tell: if the mean sits well above the median, a few power users inflate your averages and the typical user does far less. How to read it: trust the median as the everyday user; watch top user's share and the "drop the top user" number to see how fragile the headline is.
Events-per-user distribution

Event mix — what's in the total

What it is: the event count split by type. Why it matters: some events are auto-generated (opening the app, recomputing the score) and can bloat "events" without real effort. The high-signal actions — logging expenses, deposits, shares — are the ones that mean engagement.

Daily active users · 14 days

What it is: unique people who opened the app each day. How to read it: watch the shape, not any single day — a new tester wave spikes it, so a flat-to-rising line after the spike is the real signal. Healthy: line holding up or climbing once your wave-1 testers are in; a steady slide toward zero means people tried it and left.

When users are active · PH time

What it is: when people use the app, in Philippine time — distinct users by hour and weekday, split two ways: Present = anyone active (includes the daily open — when they're around), Doing = users who took a real action like logging or depositing (when they're engaged). Why it matters: the gap between the two tells you whether a time slot is just eyeballs or actual work — time your nudges to the "Doing" peaks, top-of-funnel messaging to "Present." Unique users per bucket, so one power user can't skew the shape.

Sessions — depth & quality

What it is: bursts of activity grouped with a 30-minute idle gap. Depth = events per session; quality = share of sessions with a real action (log, deposit, share) rather than a quick peek. Caveat: app-opens are throttled to once/day, so session length leans on action timestamps — read it as directional, not stopwatch. Watch: rising productive % and events/session = deeper habit; a high single-event % = peek-and-leave.

Retention cohorts — the North Star

What it is: of everyone who joined in a given week, the % still active later. D1 = came back 1–2 days after first use · D7 = active in days 7–13 · D30 = days 28–34. The gate (PRD §2): D30 ≥ 10%. That's the one number that says "this is worth building." How to read it: follow a single cohort's line down — retention always falls, but where it flattens is your true core. Faded points aren't mature yet (a fresh cohort simply hasn't lived 30 days). Trustworthy only on cohorts ≥ ~300 users — below that it's directional.

Plus fake-door funnel — by price

What it is: how many people saw the Plus offer vs. reserved it, split by the price they were shown. Intent conversion = reservers ÷ viewers. How to read it: this is a ranking tool, not a revenue forecast — clicking "reserve" costs nothing, so it overstates real purchase by roughly 3–10×. Use it only to see which price gets relatively more interest, never to project earnings.

Feature votes

What it is: which roadmap features testers asked for most. How to read it: a prioritization signal, not a promise — it tells you what to build next, but votes are cheap, so pair the top item with actual retention before committing real effort.

Lessons completed

What it is: unique users who finished each 2-minute lesson. How to read it: high completion = a lesson that hooks; a sharp drop-off at one lesson flags it as too long, boring, or confusing — a cheap thing to fix.

Category insights

What it is: which categories people actually log (validates the expanded picker), and how often they override your Need/Want default. Why it matters: a high override count on a category means your default is likely wrong for Filipinos — the direction (→Need vs →Want) tells you which way to fix it. The mix bar shows how the whole base splits their spending.

Names users created themselves. ≥3–5 users on the same name = candidate to add as an official category (review weekly/monthly).

New-feature adoption

What it is: uptake of the retention features — auto-share prompts, weekly recaps, and Coach deep-dives. How to read it: compare share prompts vs shares (are the peaks well-timed?), and watch recap / coach reach to see if the coaching actually lands. Format: count · users.

Extra-income logging

What it is: uptake of the bonus / 13th-month / side-gig logging feature, and which income sources people log. Why it matters: Filipino income is lumpy — usage validates the feature, and the source mix (e.g. lots of "side gig") tells you who your users are (gig workers vs salaried). "Split prompts" = the smart-split coaching shown after a bonus/13th-month.

Barkada (social savings)

What it is: usage of shared savings jars — jars created, people who joined, deposits made, and total pooled. Why it matters: social features are a retention lever — a user saving with friends has a reason to come back that a solo user doesn't. Rising memberships and deposits are an early sign the loop works.
